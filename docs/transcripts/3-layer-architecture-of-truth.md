Plugging raw unfiltered data straight from your source systems into a business dashboard is a recipe for immediate failure. When messy, unstructured records hit a reporting tool, the logic breaks, the numbers don't match, and the dashboard crashes. To prevent that collapse, you have to intercept those records before they reach the end user. You need a structured, tested data pipeline that cleans and organizes the information systematically.

This flowchart shows the standard three layer used in modern data tools, like DBT. Data moves strictly from left to right, starting in a raw landing zone, moving through a staging layer, then into the warehouse layer, and finally terminating in the Marts layer, where business intelligence tools actually connect. Before you build this structure, you only need three things in place: DBT installed on your machine, an active connection to a data warehouse like Snowflake or BigQuery, and your raw data sitting completely untouched in that initial landing zone.

By forcing every piece of data through these strict, isolated stages, you guarantee the insights on the other side are reliable. If there is a format error or a duplicated row, the pipeline catches it and stops it before the CEO ever opens their morning report.

The first step is the staging layer. This is a strict one-to-one cleanup environment. You're taking the exact tables from your raw landing zone and doing light formatting, like renaming columns and standardizing date types without ever changing the total number of rows. You execute this by creating a simple SQL view for each source table. You select messy, uppercase columns from the raw data and alias them into a clean, lowercase format. You then save this view using the prefix `STG_` so everyone knows immediately what level of the pipeline they are looking at.

There is a strict rule in this phase: absolutely no table joins. This layer exists to enforce the DRY principle — don't repeat yourself. If a column needs rounding or a new name, you make the fix exactly once in the staging view. Because every downstream query pulls from this staging model instead of the raw table, that single correction automatically flows through the pipeline, eliminating repeated logic errors.

Now that the data is clean, it moves into the warehouse layer. This is the structural core of your pipeline, where you finally apply business logic to those staging views and organize the records into a dimensional star schema. First, you build your dimension tables, using the prefix `DIM_`. These hold the descriptive context of your business — the nouns and adjectives — like specific customers, employee names, or product categories. Then you build your fact tables using the prefix `FCT_`. This central node stores the core measurable actions of the business — the verbs — like an invoice sent or a game played, populated with specific numbers to aggregate, like revenue and tax amounts.

To give those metrics context, the central fact table holds surrogate keys. These keys extend outward to join directly with your descriptive dimension tables, creating the classic shape of a star schema. By isolating the metrics in the center and keeping the descriptions on the outside, you keep the math clean. And because you never join a fact table directly to another fact table, you eliminate the risk of accidental many-to-many relationships that cause data duplication.

Even a perfectly designed star schema is useless if the underlying records are flawed. If the source system spits out a duplicate transaction, or forgets to assign a product ID, those errors will still ruin the final calculations. To prevent this, you apply automated data quality assertions. In DBT, you use a YAML file to declare strict rules, like requiring a surrogate key to be unique and not null, directly beneath the column definition. When you run the command `dbt test` in your terminal, the tool compiles a custom SQL script behind the scenes, sends it to your data warehouse, and actively counts the records to verify those exact conditions are met. These automated checks operate as a programmatic firewall. If a duplicate row attempts to pass through the warehouse layer, the pipeline flags the failure immediately, blocking the corrupted record from infiltrating the executive dashboards.

With the data verified, it moves to the final stage, the Marts layer. This is the presentation zone, intentionally decoupled from the technical complexity of the warehouse. Your reporting tools don't need to navigate a web of joins. Instead, you take your modular facts and dimensions and collapse them inward, merging them together to form a single wide table. You replace labels like `FACT_Sales` with plain English names like "Sales Overview", so business users know exactly what they are looking at. This final layer completely abstracts the data engineering away. You hand your business intelligence tools a flat, verified grid that is incredibly easy to filter without exposing any of the underlying pipeline mechanics.

To actually run this entire architecture, you execute a single operational trigger, the command `dbt build`. This sequence compiles the SQL, materializes staging views, builds the warehouse star schema, executes your YAML tests, and deploys the final Marts. Once the terminal flashes green, your BI tool is safely connected to a completely verified and stable Marts schema.

Implementing a strict three-layer architecture gives you total control over how records are processed. By isolating formatting, modeling, and presentation into their own dedicated zones, you elevate your data engineering from a constant state of reactive patching into a system of proactive, automated reliability.
